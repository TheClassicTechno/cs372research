NVIDIA’s current valuation appears to assume an unusually smooth continuation of extraordinary AI-driven growth. In situations where expectations are this elevated, the risk is not necessarily collapse but deceleration. If revenue growth slows from hyper-accelerated expansion to merely strong growth, valuation multiples can compress disproportionately because the premium was assigned under assumptions of continued extreme momentum. That asymmetry matters at elevated multiples.

Another concern is demand concentration. A relatively small number of hyperscalers drive incremental demand and possess significant bargaining leverage. Over time, concentrated buyers often seek pricing leverage, diversify suppliers, or internalize components of the stack through custom silicon initiatives. Even if this process is gradual, the direction of incentives is clear. Sustained pricing power becomes harder to defend when buyers have scale and strategic alternatives.

Improvements in tooling abstractions and maturation of alternative accelerators could reduce switching friction over multiple quarters. The question is not whether NVIDIA loses dominance abruptly, but whether the margin of safety implied by the current valuation is thinner than assumed.

That may well be true at the strategic level. However, infrastructure importance does not guarantee correct pricing. Even structurally important platforms can experience valuation compression if expectations outrun what can be delivered on a forward timeline.

This view would weaken if forward bookings and guidance continue to accelerate beyond the initial buildout phase and margins remain structurally elevated despite buyer concentration. Sustained multi-quarter upside surprises would directly challenge the deceleration thesis.
